02 // About Me

Md. Sajan Bishwas

I am Md. Sajan Bishwas, a PhD Student in Public Health Sciences at the University of North Carolina, Charlotte, in the Department of Epidemiology and Community Health. I hold an MPH from BRAC University's James P. Grant School of Public Health and a MSc and BSc in Public Health and Informatics from Jahangirnagar University, Bangladesh.

My doctoral research focuses on child homicide and intimate partner violence (IPV) using the National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS), examining socioeconomic and contextual determinants. I bring a mixed-methods lens — quantitative analysis in Stata and R alongside qualitative inquiry using NVivo and Dedoose.

Prior to my PhD, I served as Senior Project Officer at BRAC, leading WASH programming for Rohingya refugees on Bhasan Char Island. My trajectory has taken me from the slums of Dhaka to refugee camps in Cox's Bazar to the halls of UNC Charlotte — always with the same mission: evidence that protects vulnerable lives.

Current Doctoral Research
Child Homicide & Intimate Partner Violence in the United States

My PhD research at UNC Charlotte examines child homicide and intimate partner violence (IPV) as interconnected public health crises, using the National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS) — one of the most comprehensive surveillance datasets available in the United States.

The central focus is on understanding the socioeconomic and contextual determinants that shape risk: poverty, housing instability, neighborhood disadvantage, and access to services. I am particularly interested in how systemic inequities create conditions that elevate risk for the most vulnerable.

This research builds directly on my prior field experience with BRAC, where I oversaw child abuse reporting and child safeguarding communications for Rohingya refugees — a context that sharpened my understanding of how violence is embedded in broader structural conditions.

Past Research: Respiratory Health
Children in Waste Management — Dhaka, Bangladesh

A cross-sectional observational study investigating factors associated with the respiratory health of children working in waste management activities in Dhaka. The study was conducted as part of my MPH thesis and built on my experience with solid waste worker health monitoring at BRAC.

This research is currently under review and available as a preprint at doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-4420649/v1.

Past Research: NCDs & Cooking Fuels
Cooking Fuels and Hypertension among Women in Bangladesh

Investigated the association between solid cooking fuels and hypertension among women of reproductive age in Bangladesh, using the nationally representative BDHS 2017–2018 dataset. This study contributes to the growing literature on household air pollution as a non-communicable disease risk factor in low-income settings.
